01 — The case

Why companies build innovation labs

Intrapreneurship, formalised in the US in the 1970s by Gifford Pinchot, reached France in the 2000s. Labs are the physical expression of a simple bet: that you can manufacture innovation on purpose, inside a big organisation, faster than the market forces you to.

🚀

Develop innovation

Create a protected space where new products, services and business models can be explored away from quarterly P&L pressure.

🧲

Engage employees

Give talent a legitimate outlet for ideas — a lever for motivation, retention and internal mobility.

🔭

Identify talent

Labs surface the people who can actually ship: spotting future leaders through real projects, not slideware.

📡

Scan & signal

A visible lab signals to the market, partners and recruits that the company is future-facing — and creates an antenna for weak signals.

02 — Physical impact

What the physical space actually does

Space is not decoration — it is behavioural infrastructure. The layout decides who bumps into whom, how loud ideas get, and whether a prototype dies in a drawer or on a wall everyone walks past.

Proximity

Collisions on purpose

Allen's curve: communication drops sharply beyond ~50m between desks. Labs cluster mixed teams to force "productive collisions" (the MIT Building 20 / Pixar atrium effect).

Visibility

Work on the walls

Writable surfaces, pin-up zones and prototype shelves make progress visible. Visible work invites feedback and creates social accountability.

Modularity

Furniture that moves

Casters, foldable walls, reconfigurable rooms. The space must change as fast as the project — fixed layouts freeze old assumptions.

Zoning

Loud vs. focus

A good lab separates "divergent / noisy" zones (workshops, build) from "convergent / quiet" zones (deep work, calls). Acoustic failure kills both.

Threshold

A door that means something

Crossing into the lab should feel different from the corridor. Identity and ritual at the entrance shift behaviour ("here, we prototype").

Making

From talk to thing

A maker corner (3D print, cardboard, electronics) lowers the cost of trying. Cheap making = more shots on goal.

03 — Programs

The program archetypes

A lab is the venue; the program is the engine. Most companies run one or a blend of these. Each has a different ROI logic — match the program to the strategic intent, not the fashion.

Intrapreneurship program

04 — People

The roles that make a lab work

Labs fail on org design more often than on ideas. These are the roles you should look for (or ask about) at Orange and Sanofi.

Sponsor

Executive sponsor

A C-level owner who protects budget & air-cover. Without one, the lab is an orphan.

Lead

Lab director

Runs the portfolio, the rituals and the politics. Half-builder, half-diplomat. (e.g. Sylvain Grivel, 14 Bis)

Maker

Intrapreneur

The employee betting their time on an idea. Needs a real mandate and a way back if it fails.

Coach

Venture coach

Lean / design-thinking facilitator who keeps teams testing, not polishing.

Bridge

Business-unit liaison

Connects projects to the core business so winners can scale (the hardest handoff).

Tech

Solutions / engineer

Turns concepts into working prototypes fast. (e.g. Julien Manivong, Orange)

Design

UX / service designer

Keeps the user at the centre — research, journeys, usability.

Capital

Venture / finance partner

Owns funding gates and the ROI story to the board.

05 — Strategic fit

Success = strategy, not ping-pong tables

The single biggest predictor of lab survival is whether it is wired to corporate strategy and coexists with the company's other innovation vehicles — instead of competing with them for budget and credit.

The three horizons it must serve

Map every lab project to McKinsey's Three Horizons so the board sees balance, not just shiny H3 bets:

  • H1 — optimise the core (incremental, fast payback)
  • H2 — adjacent growth (new segments/models, 2–4 yrs)
  • H3 — transformational bets (option value, high risk)

A lab that only does H3 looks irrelevant; one that only does H1 looks redundant. Aim for a deliberate portfolio mix.

Coexistence with other innovation engines

The lab is one of several vehicles. Define clear lanes so they reinforce rather than collide:

  • Lab / incubator — explore & de-risk new bets
  • R&D — deep technical capability
  • Corporate VC — invest in external startups
  • M&A — buy what you can't build
  • Open innovation / accelerator — partner & source
  • Continuous improvement — the core's own engine

Map handoffs: who takes a validated concept to scale? The "valley of death" between lab and BU is where most ideas die.

5 conditions for survival — the audit questions to ask on site

01 MANDATE

Is there a clear strategic mandate & executive sponsor?

02 METRICS

03 SCALE PATH

06 — Bottom-up

Promoting bottom-up intrapreneurial innovation

Top-down labs generate decks. Bottom-up labs generate ventures. Here's the playbook for getting ideas to bubble up from the 95% of employees who never set foot in the lab.

Make the call open & recurring

Run regular idea calls / hackathons with a clear theme tied to strategy. Open to everyone, not just R&D. Predictable cadence beats one-off events.

Give time, not just permission

Protected time (the "20% time" idea, Gore's "dabble time") signals the company is serious. Permission without time is theatre.

Lower the cost of a first step

Micro-grants, a maker space, and lightweight pitch templates so an employee can test an idea in a week, not a quarter.

Stage-gate with real money & real kills

Fund in tranches against evidence. Celebrate good kills — a killed-with-learning project is a win, not a failure.

Protect the intrapreneur's career

A guaranteed role to return to, recognition, and equity-like upside. Fear of career suicide is the #1 reason employees don't pitch.

Show the wins loudly

Visible alumni and shipped projects create a flywheel: stories recruit the next cohort better than any internal memo.
